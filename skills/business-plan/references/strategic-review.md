## SWOT Analysis

### Internal Factors

**Strengths** (Internal, Positive)
- What do we do better than competitors?
- What unique resources do we have?
- What do customers say we are good at?
- What is our unfair advantage?

Probe for: core competencies, proprietary technology/IP, strong customer relationships, talented team, efficient operations, brand reputation.

**Weaknesses** (Internal, Negative)
- Where do we underperform?
- What resources are we lacking?
- What do customers complain about?
- Where are we vulnerable?

Probe for: capability gaps, resource constraints, operational inefficiencies, product limitations, customer experience issues, organizational problems.

### External Factors

**Opportunities** (External, Positive)
- What market trends benefit us?
- What competitor weaknesses can we exploit?
- What unmet needs exist?
- What regulatory or technology changes help us?

Probe for: growing segments, underserved needs, competitor missteps, technology enablers, partnership possibilities, geographic expansion.

**Threats** (External, Negative)
- What market trends hurt us?
- What are competitors doing?
- What could disrupt our business?
- What regulatory or economic risks exist?

Probe for: new competitors, changing preferences, technology disruption, regulatory changes, economic headwinds, supply chain vulnerabilities.

### SWOT Quality Rules

- Every factor must be **specific and evidence-based** (not generic like "strong team" — say what makes the team strong and why it matters).
- Each factor should be **actionable** — if you cannot do anything about it, it does not belong in the SWOT.
- Limit to **4-6 items per quadrant**, ranked by impact.

## TOWS Strategic Options

Combine SWOT factors to generate strategic options:

| | Strengths (S) | Weaknesses (W) |
|---|---|---|
| **Opportunities (O)** | **SO Strategies** (Maxi-Maxi): Use strengths to capture opportunities | **WO Strategies** (Mini-Maxi): Address weaknesses to capture opportunities |
| **Threats (T)** | **ST Strategies** (Maxi-Mini): Use strengths to mitigate threats | **WT Strategies** (Mini-Mini): Defensive — minimize weaknesses and threats |

**SO (Aggressive/Growth)**: How can we use [strength] to exploit [opportunity]?
**WO (Developmental)**: What weaknesses prevent us from capturing [opportunity]?
**ST (Defensive)**: How can we use [strength] to defend against [threat]?
**WT (Survival)**: What must we fix to survive [threat]?

## Moat Analysis

Sustainable competitive advantages that protect the business from competition. Identify which moats exist and how strong they are.

### Types of Moats

**1. Network Effects** — Value increases as more users join.
- Strong: marketplace, social network, payment platform
- Weak: content platform, community
- Key question: Does each new user make the product more valuable for existing users?

**2. Switching Costs** — Pain of changing to a competitor.
- High: enterprise software, banking, deeply integrated tools
- Low: consumer apps, commodity products
- Key question: What would a customer lose by switching?

**3. Cost Advantages** — Structural ability to operate at lower cost.
- Scale economies (unit cost decreases with volume)
- Process innovation (proprietary efficiency)
- Favorable access to inputs (location, partnerships)

**4. Intangible Assets** — Hard to replicate advantages.
- Brand recognition and trust
- Patents and intellectual property
- Regulatory licenses
- Proprietary data sets

**5. Efficient Scale** — Market too small for multiple competitors.
- Niche dominance (the best at one specific thing)
- Geographic monopoly (only provider in a region)

A business does not need all five moats. One strong moat is enough if it is durable. The critical question is: what stops a well-funded competitor from replicating what you do?

## Strategic Options Evaluation

### Scoring Framework

For each strategic option:

| Criteria | Weight | Score (1-5) | Weighted |
|----------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Strategic Fit | 20% | | |
| Financial Return | 25% | | |
| Risk Level | 20% | | |
| Feasibility | 20% | | |
| Time to Value | 15% | | |
| **Total** | 100% | | |

### Key Questions per Criterion

**Strategic Fit**: Does this align with mission? Leverage strengths? Fit risk appetite?
**Financial Return**: What is the expected ROI? Payback period? Revenue potential?
**Risk Level**: What could go wrong? How recoverable? (Invert the score: low risk = high score)
**Feasibility**: Do we have the capabilities? What needs to change? Resource requirements?
**Time to Value**: How long until results? Can we afford to wait?

### Risk Matrix

For each strategic option, plot key risks:

| Risk | Probability (L/M/H) | Impact (L/M/H) | Response |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|----------|
| | | | Accept / Mitigate / Transfer / Avoid |

## Strategic Review Process

### Phase 1: Situation Assessment
1. Review mission, vision, values
2. Analyze current performance vs goals
3. Document key assumptions about the business
4. Identify the strategic questions this review should answer

### Phase 2: External Analysis
1. Market trends (PESTEL — see `market-analysis.md`)
2. Industry dynamics (Porter's Five Forces)
3. Competitive landscape
4. Customer insights

### Phase 3: Internal Analysis
1. Financial performance (see `financial-analysis.md`)
2. Operational capabilities
3. Resource assessment
4. Culture and organizational health

### Phase 4: SWOT Synthesis
1. Compile SWOT factors from Phases 2 and 3
2. Prioritize by impact
3. Generate TOWS strategies
4. Identify 2-4 strategic options

### Phase 5: Strategy Selection
1. Evaluate options using the scoring framework
2. Assess risks for top options
3. Define success metrics
4. Build implementation roadmap with milestones

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] SWOT factors are specific, evidence-based, and actionable
- [ ] Competitive analysis based on research, not assumptions
- [ ] Strategic options are genuinely different (not variations of the same idea)
- [ ] Recommendations tied clearly to the analysis
- [ ] Risks identified with realistic mitigations
- [ ] Implementation priorities are concrete and achievable
- [ ] Success metrics are measurable and time-bound
- [ ] Moat assessment is honest about weaknesses
