Create comprehensive, well-structured business plans adapted to stage and purpose.

## Information Gathering

Before writing, establish what you know and what you need. Adapt depth to stage.

### The Business
1. What problem are you solving? (Customer pain point — be specific)
2. What is your solution? (Product/service description from the user's perspective)
3. Who is your customer? (Target segment, buyer persona)
4. What is your business model? (How you make money)
5. What stage are you at? (Idea, pre-revenue, early revenue, growth, mature)

### The Market
1. Market size (TAM/SAM/SOM — see `market-analysis.md`)
2. Growth rate (historical and projected)
3. Key trends (tailwinds and headwinds)
4. Competitive landscape (direct and indirect competitors)

### The Financials
1. Current revenue (if any)
2. Cost structure (fixed vs variable)
3. Funding to date (sources and amounts)
4. Funding needs (amount and intended use)

### The Purpose
1. Who is the audience for this plan? (self, investors, partners, bank)
2. What decisions should this plan inform?
3. What format do they expect?

## Plan Structure

### 1. Executive Summary (1 page) — Write This LAST

Even though it appears first, write this after everything else is done. It distills the entire plan into a page.

Include exactly these elements:
1. What the business does (one sentence)
2. The problem it solves and for whom (two sentences max)
3. The solution and why it is different (two sentences)
4. The market opportunity (one sentence with a number)
5. Business model summary (one sentence)
6. Traction to date (one sentence — skip if pre-idea)
7. What you need or are asking for (one sentence)

**Test**: If someone reads only this page, they should understand the entire business.

### 2. Problem & Opportunity (1-2 pages)

This is the heart of the plan. If this section is weak, everything after it is built on sand.

**The Problem:**
- Describe the specific problem with concrete detail. Who feels it? When? How often?
- Quantify the pain: time wasted, money lost, opportunity cost.
- Back it up with evidence: customer quotes, forum posts, market data. Not just opinion.
- Example: "Freelancers in this space spend an average of 6 hours/week on [X], costing them ~$15K/year in lost billable time."

**The Solution:**
- Describe the user experience, not just features. What happens from the customer's perspective?
- Before and after: the painful status quo vs. life with your solution.
- Core innovation: the non-obvious insight that makes this work where others failed.

**Why Now:**
- What changed in technology, regulation, market behavior, or customer expectations?
- Why has this not been done before? Why is now different?
- This matters for investor audiences — it creates urgency and explains timing.

### 3. Market Analysis (2-3 pages)

See `market-analysis.md` for detailed methodology. Summarize into:

- **Market size**: TAM, SAM, SOM with sources and methodology
- **Market trends**: Top 3 trends and how they impact your opportunity
- **Customer segments**: 1-2 primary personas with key characteristics
- **Target segment size**: How many target customers exist and how reachable they are

Every claim needs a source or clear methodology.

### 4. Competitive Landscape (1-2 pages)

- Comparison matrix of top 3-5 competitors across dimensions that matter most
- Your competitive wedge — the position competitors do not occupy
- Table stakes you must match
- Gaps you fill that competitors miss
- Honest assessment of competitor strengths — dismissing them weakens your plan

### 5. Business Model (1-2 pages)

- Revenue streams: what you charge, how, at what price points
- Customer acquisition: how customers find you (channels and strategy)
- Sales flow: discovery → evaluation → purchase
- Unit economics: CAC, LTV, payback period, contribution margin (even if estimated)
- For early stage: state assumptions clearly and show how you will validate them

### 6. Go-to-Market Strategy (1-2 pages)

- Launch plan and initial customer acquisition strategy
- Marketing channels: which 2-3 channels first and why
- Content strategy (if applicable)
- Sales approach: direct outreach, inbound, self-serve, enterprise
- Customer acquisition targets tied to revenue goals
- Partnership opportunities

### 7. Operations Plan (1-2 pages)

- Product/service delivery: how does fulfillment work end-to-end?
- Technology stack and infrastructure
- Key processes: 3-5 recurring processes that keep the business running
- Automation plan: what can be automated, with what tools
- For solopreneurs: outsourcing triggers (revenue level, time constraint)
- For teams: organizational structure and key hires needed

### 8. Team (1 page)

- Founder backgrounds with relevant experience
- Key team members and their roles
- Advisory board (if any)
- Key hires needed and when
- Why this team for this problem
- Team gaps acknowledged honestly

For solopreneurs: focus on your relevant expertise, your network, and your plan for when you need to grow beyond yourself.

### 9. Financial Projections (3-5 pages)

See [Financial Projection Guidelines](#financial-projection-guidelines) below and `financial-analysis.md` for detailed frameworks.

- Assumptions summary (clearly stated and reasonable)
- Year 1 monthly P&L, Years 2-3 quarterly
- Cash flow projection
- Key metrics dashboard (MRR growth, CAC, LTV, burn rate, runway)
- Break-even analysis
- Funding requirements and milestones
- Conservative and optimistic scenarios

### 10. Risk Assessment (1 page)

For each risk:
1. What the risk is
2. Likelihood (Low / Medium / High)
3. Impact (Low / Medium / High)
4. Mitigation plan

**Common risks to cover:**
- Single-person / key-person dependency
- Platform/vendor dependency
- Customer concentration (>20% from one source)
- Market timing (too early or too late)
- Pricing risk
- Competition from well-funded players
- Regulatory / legal changes
- Cash flow timing mismatches

### 11. Appendices (as needed)

- Detailed financial model
- Market research sources
- Technical specifications
- Customer testimonials or LOIs
- Patent/IP summary

## Financial Projection Guidelines

### Revenue Build-Up (Bottom-Up)

Always build revenue bottom-up — it is more credible than top-down.

```
Number of potential customers you can reach
× Conversion rate
× Average contract value
× Purchase frequency
= Revenue
```

Build monthly for Year 1, quarterly for Years 2-3.

### Cost Structure

**Fixed Costs:**
- Salaries and benefits (or contractor costs for solopreneurs)
- Rent and facilities (if applicable)
- Software and tools
- Insurance and professional fees

**Variable Costs:**
- Cost of goods / direct delivery costs
- Sales commissions
- Payment processing
- Customer support (scaled to customer count)
- Marketing spend (tied to acquisition targets)

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| MRR Growth | (MRR_current - MRR_previous) / MRR_previous | Context-dependent |
| CAC | Total acquisition spend / New customers | Lower than LTV/3 |
| LTV | ARPU × Gross Margin × (1 / Churn Rate) | >3× CAC |
| LTV:CAC | LTV / CAC | >3:1 |
| Payback Period | CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin) | <12 months ideal |
| Gross Margin | (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue | Industry-dependent |
| Burn Rate | (Cash_start - Cash_end) / Months | Sustainable to break-even |
| Runway | Current Cash / Monthly Burn Rate | >12 months comfort zone |

### Key Thresholds

- **Break-even**: What revenue level covers all costs?
- **Runway**: How many months until cash runs out at current burn?
- **Cash flow timing**: Are there months where expenses spike?

### Honesty Rules

- Projections are guesses — label them as such.
- Include conservative and optimistic scenarios. The conservative scenario must still be a viable business.
- Show the math behind every number. No unexplained round numbers.
- Benchmark assumptions against industry data where possible.
- If an assumption feels aggressive, say so and explain what would need to be true.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Executive summary stands alone
- [ ] All claims backed by data or logic
- [ ] Financial projections internally consistent
- [ ] Revenue projections show bottom-up math
- [ ] Competitive analysis is honest (not dismissive)
- [ ] Risks acknowledged with specific mitigations
- [ ] Clear ask and use of funds (if applicable)
- [ ] Team gaps acknowledged
- [ ] Assumptions labeled and tracked separately from facts
- [ ] Output format matches the stated audience and purpose
