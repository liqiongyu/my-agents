## Market Sizing

### TAM / SAM / SOM

**Total Addressable Market (TAM)** — Everyone who could potentially buy.
- Global or regional depending on scope
- Use both top-down and bottom-up for triangulation

**Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)** — Portion you can realistically reach.
- Constrained by: geography, segments, channels, capabilities
- Typically 10-40% of TAM

**Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)** — What you can capture in 3-5 years.
- Based on: competition, growth rate, sales capacity
- Typically 1-10% of SAM

### Calculation Methods

**Top-Down** (from industry reports):
```
Industry Revenue (from reports)
× % relevant segment
× % accessible geography
= TAM

TAM × % reachable segment = SAM
SAM × realistic market share = SOM
```

**Bottom-Up** (more credible, preferred):
```
Number of potential customers in target segment
× Average contract value / purchase value
× Purchase frequency per year
= TAM

Apply realistic reach constraints = SAM
Apply competitive dynamics = SOM
```

Always show both methods when possible. Divergence between top-down and bottom-up reveals assumptions worth examining.

## Competitive Analysis

### Competitor Categories

**Direct Competitors** — Same product, same customer, same problem.
**Indirect Competitors** — Different product, same customer, same problem.
**Substitutes** — Customer could solve the problem another way (including doing nothing).

Never say "we have no competition." Doing nothing is always a competitor.

### Competitive Matrix

| Criteria | Weight | You | Comp A | Comp B | Comp C |
|----------|--------|-----|--------|--------|--------|

| Integration | 15% | | | | |
| **Weighted Total** | 100% | | | | |

Score 1-5 for each dimension, multiply by weight.

### Competitor Deep-Dive Questions

For each key competitor:
1. **Positioning**: How do they describe themselves?
2. **Target Customer**: Who are they selling to?
3. **Pricing**: What do they charge? What model?
4. **Strengths**: What are they genuinely good at?
5. **Weaknesses**: Where do they fail?
6. **Trajectory**: Growing, flat, or declining?
7. **Resources**: How well-funded or profitable?
8. **Strategy**: Where are they heading? (Check job postings for clues)

## Porter's Five Forces

Rate each force as High / Medium / Low and explain why.

### 1. Competitive Rivalry
- Number of competitors
- Industry growth rate (slow growth = more rivalry)
- Product differentiation (commodity = more rivalry)
- Exit barriers
- Fixed cost structure

### 2. Threat of New Entrants
- Capital requirements to enter
- Economies of scale
- Access to distribution channels
- Regulatory barriers
- Brand loyalty and switching costs

### 3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
- Supplier concentration
- Switching costs to change suppliers
- Threat of forward integration
- Importance of volume to supplier

### 4. Bargaining Power of Buyers
- Buyer concentration
- Switching costs for buyers
- Price sensitivity
- Threat of backward integration
- Information availability

### 5. Threat of Substitutes
- Availability of substitute products
- Price-performance trade-off
- Switching costs
- Buyer propensity to switch

**Overall Industry Attractiveness**: Synthesize the five forces into a summary assessment. More forces favorable = more attractive industry.

Focus on factors that directly affect the business. Do not fill every cell — empty cells are fine if a factor is not relevant.

### Key Trend Categories
1. **Demand Drivers**: What is increasing the need for this solution?
2. **Technology Shifts**: What enables new approaches?
3. **Regulatory Changes**: What is newly required or restricted?
4. **Customer Behavior**: How are buying patterns changing?
5. **Competitive Dynamics**: Consolidation? New entrants?

## Research Sources

### Primary (highest credibility)
- Customer interviews and surveys
- Expert calls (GLG, AlphaSights)
- Industry events and conferences
- Sales team feedback (for existing businesses)

### Secondary
- Industry reports: Statista, IBISWorld, Gartner, CB Insights
- Company filings: SEC (US), Companies House (UK), EDGAR
- News and press releases
- Academic research
- Government statistics and census data

### Competitor Intelligence
- Website and product analysis
- Pricing pages (use Wayback Machine for history)
- Job postings (reveal strategic priorities)
- LinkedIn for team size and hiring velocity
- Review sites: G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, App Store
- Patent filings
- Crunchbase / PitchBook for funding data

When using web search to gather data, cite sources and note the date of the data. Stale data is worse than no data if it creates false confidence.
